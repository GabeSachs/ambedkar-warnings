Constituent Assembly of India · 25 November 1949
Ambedkar's
Six Warnings
The closing speech — and what it argues about Indian democracy

On 25 November 1949, the day before India's Constitution was adopted, B.R. Ambedkar rose to give his closing address to the Constituent Assembly. He had chaired the Drafting Committee for nearly three years. The speech contains institutional reflection, pride in the Constitution's making, and warning.

This project presents Ambedkar's full speech from the official transcript alongside analysis of what each passage meant, what Ambedkar was responding to, and why it matters. The “six warnings” structure is this project's analytical framing, not a label Ambedkar himself used. Click any highlighted passage to open its analysis.

Ambedkar's Six Warnings
Constituent Assembly · 25 November 1949 · Vol. XI
WARNINGS:
Speaker: The Honorable Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, Chairman of the Drafting Committee
Date: Friday, 25 November 1949 · Constitution Hall, New Delhi
Context: The Constitution would be formally adopted the following day. Ambedkar had chaired the seven-member Drafting Committee since August 1947. This was his final address to the Assembly as its architect.
Six passages are highlighted. Click any to open its analysis in the right panel. Press keys 1–6 to navigate directly between warnings.

[The transcript opens with Ambedkar's account of the Drafting Committee's work and tributes to colleagues. The speech excerpted here begins where Ambedkar turns from the past to the future.]

Here I could have ended. But my mind is so full of the future of our country that I feel I ought to take this occasion to give expression to some of my reflections thereon.

On 26th January 1950, India will be an independent country. What would happen to her independence? Will she maintain her independence or will she lose it again? This is the first thought that comes to my mind. It is not that India was never an independent country. The point is that she once lost the independence she had. Will she lose it a second time? It is this thought which makes me most anxious for the future.

What perturbs me greatly is the fact that not only India has once before lost her independence, but she lost it by the infidelity and treachery of some of her own people. In the invasion of Sind by Mahommed-Bin-Kasim, the military commanders of King Dahar accepted bribes from the agents of Mahommed-Bin-Kasim and refused to fight on the side of their King. It was Jaichand who invited Mahommed Gohri to invade India and fight against Prithvi Raj and promised him the help of himself and the Solanki Kings. When Shivaji was fighting for the liberation of Hindus, the other Maratha noblemen and the Rajput Kings were fighting the battle on the side of Moghul Emperors. When the British were trying to destroy the Sikh Rulers, Gulab Singh, their principal commander sat silent and did not help to save the Sikh Kingdom. In 1857, when a large part of India had declared a war of independence against the British, the Sikhs stood and watched the event as silent spectators.

Will history repeat itself? It is this thought which fills me with anxiety. This anxiety is deepened by the realization of the fact that in addition to our old enemies in the form of castes and creeds we are going to have many political parties with diverse and opposing political creeds. Will Indians place the country above their creed or will they place creed above country? I do not know. But this much is certain that if the parties place creed above country, our independence will be put in jeopardy a second time and probably be lost for ever. This eventuality we must all resolutely guard against. We must be determined to defend our independence with the last drop of our blood.

On the 26th of January 1950, India would be a democratic country in the sense that India from that day would have a government of the people, by the people and for the people. The same thought comes to my mind. What would happen to her democratic Constitution? Will she be able to maintain it or will she lose it again. This is the second thought that comes to my mind and makes me as anxious as the first.

It is not that India did not know what is Democracy. There was a time when India was studded with republics, and even where there were monarchies, they were either elected or limited. They were never absolute. It is not that India did not know Parliaments or Parliamentary Procedure. A study of the Buddhist Bhikshu Sanghas discloses that not only there were Parliaments — for the Sanghas were nothing but Parliaments — but the Sanghas knew and observed all the rules of Parliamentary Procedure known to modern times. They had rules regarding seating arrangements, rules regarding Motions, Resolution, Quorum, Whip, Counting of Votes, Voting by Ballot, Censure Motion, Regularization, Res Judicata, etc.

This democratic system India lost. Will she lose it a second time? I do not know. But it is quite possible in a country like India — where democracy from its long disuse must be regarded as something quite new — there is danger of democracy giving place to dictatorship. It is quite possible for this new born democracy to retain its form but give place to dictatorship in fact. If there is a landslide, the danger of the second possibility becoming actuality is much greater.

If we wish to maintain democracy not merely in form, but also in fact, what must we do? The first thing in my judgment we must do is to hold fast to constitutional methods of achieving our social and economic objectives. It means we must abandon the bloody methods of revolution. It means that we must abandon the method of civil disobedience, non-cooperation and satyagraha. When there was no way left for constitutional methods for achieving economic and social objectives, there was a great deal of justification for unconstitutional methods. But where constitutional methods are open, there can be no justification for these unconstitutional methods. These methods are nothing but the Grammar of Anarchy and the sooner they are abandoned, the better for us.

The second thing we must do is to observe the caution which John Stuart Mill has given to all who are interested in the maintenance of democracy, namely, not "to lay their liberties at the feet of even a great man, or to trust him with powers which enable him to subvert their institutions". There is nothing wrong in being grateful to great men who have rendered life-long services to the country. But there are limits to gratefulness. As has been well said by the Irish Patriot Daniel O'Connell, no man can be grateful at the cost of his honor, no woman can be grateful at the cost of her chastity and no nation can be grateful at the cost of its liberty. This caution is far more necessary in the case of India than in the case of any other country. For in India, Bhakti or what may be called the path of devotion or hero-worship, plays a part in its politics unequalled in magnitude by the part it plays in the politics of any other country in the world. Bhakti in religion may be a road to the salvation of the soul. But in politics, Bhakti or hero-worship is a sure road to degradation and to eventual dictatorship.

The third thing we must do is not to be content with mere political democracy. We must make our political democracy a social democracy as well. Political democracy cannot last unless there lies at the base of it social democracy. What does social democracy mean? It means a way of life which recognizes liberty, equality and fraternity as the principles of life. These principles of liberty, equality and fraternity are not to be treated as separate items in a trinity. They form a union of trinity in the sense that to divorce one from the other is to defeat the very purpose of democracy. Liberty cannot be divorced from equality, equality cannot be divorced from liberty. Nor can liberty and equality be divorced from fraternity. Without equality, liberty would produce the supremacy of the few over the many. Equality without liberty would kill individual initiative. Without fraternity, liberty and equality could not become a natural course of things. It would require a constable to enforce them.

We must begin by acknowledging the fact that there is complete absence of two things in Indian Society. One of these is equality. On the social plane, we have in India a society based on the principle of graded inequality which means elevation for some and degradation for others. On the economic plane, we have a society in which there are some who have immense wealth as against many who live in abject poverty. On the 26th of January 1950, we are going to enter into a life of contradictions. In politics we will have equality and in social and economic life we will have inequality. In politics we will be recognizing the principle of one man one vote and one vote one value. In our social and economic life, we shall, by reason of our social and economic structure, continue to deny the principle of one man one value. How long shall we continue to live this life of contradictions? How long shall we continue to deny equality in our social and economic life? If we continue to deny it for long, we will do so only by putting our political democracy in peril. We must remove this contradiction at the earliest possible moment or else those who suffer from inequality will blow up the structure of political democracy which this Assembly has so laboriously built up.

The second thing we are wanting in is recognition of the principle of fraternity. What does fraternity mean? Fraternity means a sense of common brotherhood of all Indians — if Indians being one people. It is the principle which gives unity and solidarity to social life. It is a difficult thing to achieve. How difficult it is, can be realized from the story related by James Bryce in his volume on American Commonwealth about the United States of America.

The story is — I propose to recount it in the words of Bryce himself — that "Some years ago the American Protestant Episcopal Church was occupied at its triennial Convention in revising its liturgy. It was thought desirable to introduce among the short sentence prayers a prayer for the whole people, and an eminent New England divine proposed the words 'O Lord, bless our nation.' Accepted one afternoon, on the spur of the moment, the sentence was brought up next day for reconsideration, when so many objections were raised by the laity to the word 'nation,' as importing too definite a recognition of national unity, that it was dropped, and instead there were adopted the words 'O Lord, bless these United States.'"

There was so little solidarity in the U.S.A. at the time when this incident occurred that the people of America did not think that they were a nation. If the people of the United States could not feel that they were a nation, how difficult it is for Indians to think that they are a nation. I remember the days when politically-minded Indians resented the expression "the people of India." They preferred the expression "the Indian nation." I am of opinion that in believing that we are a nation, we are cherishing a great delusion. How can people divided into several thousands of castes be a nation? The sooner we realize that we are not as yet a nation in the social and psychological sense of the world, the better for us. For then only we shall realize the necessity of becoming a nation and seriously think of ways and means of realizing the goal. The realization of this goal is going to be very difficult — far more difficult than it has been in the United States. The United States has no caste problem. In India there are castes. The castes are anti-national. In the first place because they bring about separation in social life. They are anti-national also because they generate jealousy and antipathy between caste and caste. But we must overcome all these difficulties if we wish to become a nation in reality. For fraternity can be a fact only when there is a nation. Without fraternity equality and liberty will be no deeper than coats of paint.

These are my reflections about the tasks that lie ahead of us. They may not be very pleasant to some. But there can be no gainsaying that political power in this country has too long been the monopoly of a few and the many are not only beasts of burden, but also beasts of prey. This monopoly has not merely deprived them of their chance of betterment, it has sapped them of what may be called the significance of life. These down-trodden classes are tired of being governed. They are impatient to govern themselves. This urge for self-realization in the down-trodden classes must not be allowed to devolve into a class struggle or class war. It would lead to a division of the House. That would indeed be a day of disaster. For, as has been well said by Abraham Lincoln, a House divided against itself cannot stand very long. Therefore the sooner room is made for the realization of their aspiration, the better for the few, the better for the country, the better for the maintenance for its independence and the better for the continuance of its democratic structure. This can only be done by the establishment of equality and fraternity in all spheres of life. That is why I have laid so much stress on them.

I do not wish to weary the House any further. Independence is no doubt a matter of joy. But let us not forget that this independence has thrown on us great responsibilities. By independence, we have lost the excuse of blaming the British for anything going wrong. If hereafter things go wrong, we will have nobody to blame except ourselves.

There is great danger of things going wrong. Times are fast changing. People including our own are being moved by new ideologies. They are getting tired of Government by the people. They are prepared to have Government for the people and are indifferent whether it is Government of the people and by the people. If we wish to preserve the Constitution in which we have sought to enshrine the principle of Government of the people, for the people and by the people, let us resolve not to be tardy in the recognition of the evils that lie across our path and which induce people to prefer Government for the people to Government by the people, nor to be weak in our initiative to remove them. That is the only way to serve the country. I know of no better.

[End of address. The Assembly adjourned until 26 November 1949, when the Constitution was formally adopted.]

WARNING ONE
Castes and creeds above country
The warning
"Will Indians place the country above their creed or will they place creed above country? I do not know. But this much is certain that if the parties place creed above country, our independence will be put in jeopardy a second time and probably be lost for ever."
Constituent Assembly Debates, Vol. XI, 25 November 1949
The history lesson before the warning

Ambedkar does not open with the warning. He opens with five examples. Mahommed-Bin-Kasim's invasion succeeded because King Dahar's own commanders took his money. Prithvi Raj fell because Jaichand sent him an invitation to be invaded. Shivaji fought while Maratha and Rajput nobles fought alongside his enemies. Gulab Singh sat still while the British destroyed the Sikh kingdom. The Sikhs watched 1857 happen.

Each example is the same thing: an Indian community calculating that its own survival was better served by siding with the invader than by defending the whole. The community is different each time — Hindu, Muslim, Sikh — but the calculation is identical. Ambedkar lines these up not to make a moral point about treachery but to establish a pattern. Across twelve centuries, this is what Indian political life does under pressure. It fragments along community lines. The warning that follows is a historical induction, not a prediction.

The room he was speaking in

The speech was delivered fourteen months after Partition. Several people in that room had watched their cities burn the year before. The Assembly Ambedkar addressed was itself a consequence of creed being placed above country — it no longer represented all of India, only the part that had not become Pakistan. The question of whether Indians would place country above creed had just been answered at enormous cost by millions of people on both sides of a border that had not existed two years earlier.

Ambedkar does not mention Partition. He ends his list of examples in 1857 and moves straight to the warning. The gap does not need to be filled.

What the warning actually says

This is not only a moral argument. Ambedkar is not simply saying community loyalty is wrong. He is also making a structural claim: when parties organise around community identities, the incentive to serve any interest larger than the community's own is removed. The process is competitive and self-reinforcing: once one party mobilises a community, the others must do the same. No individual party needs to be malicious. The result, regardless of anyone's intentions, is a political system that rewards communal service and has no mechanism for producing nationally coherent policy.

The phrase "lost for ever" is worth holding onto. Not damaged. Not set back. Lost for ever — the same outcome as every example in his prologue, each of which ended in generations of subjugation. He is treating this as existential, not ordinary. And then, immediately after that, he says: I do not know. After building a case from five centuries of evidence, he refuses the conclusion. It is a question, not a prediction. Whether the sixth instance will follow the previous five, he honestly cannot say.

WARNING TWO
Democracy in form, dictatorship in fact
The warning
"It is quite possible for this new born democracy to retain its form but give place to dictatorship in fact. If there is a landslide, the danger of the second possibility becoming actuality is much greater."
Constituent Assembly Debates, Vol. XI, 25 November 1949
The distinction

Ambedkar separates two things that are easy to conflate: the forms of democracy and its substance. The forms are the visible machinery — elections held on schedule, parliament in session, a constitution on the books, courts nominally functioning. The substance is what that machinery actually does: whether elections produce real accountability, whether parliament constrains the executive, whether rights are enforced in practice. His point is that the first can continue indefinitely while the second disappears, and that the continuation of the first will make it very difficult for anyone to say precisely when the second went.

Ambedkar is describing not a collapse but a slow substitution — the forms kept as a legitimating surface while the substance underneath is replaced. The surface is not incidental to this process. It is essential. As long as elections are held, the claim that democracy has ended becomes hard to sustain. That is exactly why this kind of failure is so durable.

Why a landslide

Democratic institutions do not run themselves. Judicial independence, a free press, opposition rights — none of these are self-sustaining. They persist because it is costly for those in power to dismantle them, and the primary source of that cost is competition. A government that might lose the next election has reason to respect the rules. A government that expects to win indefinitely does not. A landslide removes the competitive pressure that keeps the constraints functional.

Congress won 364 of 489 Lok Sabha seats in the first general election, held in 1951–52. Ambedkar was making a structural argument before that result was known, but its force is sharpened by what followed: the party he had spent his career distrusting governed for decades with exactly the kind of dominant majority his warning describes.

The warning's real difficulty

This warning produces no threshold and no test. A coup has a date. Gradual institutional erosion does not — each action is technically legal, each norm violation is individually defensible, each appointment is within the rules. There is no moment at which it becomes unambiguous. Someone who says the threshold has been crossed will be called alarmist. Someone who says it hasn't will be told they aren't paying attention. Ambedkar names the danger without providing a way to verify when it has become real. That is not a gap in the argument. It is an accurate description of how this kind of failure works.

WARNING THREE
The Grammar of Anarchy
The warning
"We must abandon the method of civil disobedience, non-cooperation and satyagraha. When there was no way left for constitutional methods for achieving economic and social objectives, there was a great deal of justification for unconstitutional methods. But where constitutional methods are open, there can be no justification for these unconstitutional methods. These methods are nothing but the Grammar of Anarchy and the sooner they are abandoned, the better for us."
Constituent Assembly Debates, Vol. XI, 25 November 1949
What he was asking people to do

Civil disobedience, non-cooperation, satyagraha — these were not abstract tactics. They were how India had won its independence. The people sitting in that room had been imprisoned for using them. Some had built their political identities around the idea that courting arrest, meeting violence with nonviolent resistance, refusing to cooperate with a system you had not chosen, was the highest form of political action available to a colonised people.

Ambedkar stood up the day before the Constitution was adopted and told all of them to give those up permanently. His argument is simple: unconstitutional methods were justified when no constitutional channel existed. The Constitution creates those channels. The justification is therefore gone. To keep using extra-constitutional pressure after legitimate channels exist is to say your goals matter more than the framework that protects everyone else's rights — including your opponents'. And once you establish that sufficiently just causes justify bypassing the Constitution, you have made that principle available to everyone who believes their cause is just. There is no principled way to distinguish your civil disobedience from theirs.

Who the warning was aimed at

In 1932, Gandhi fasted in prison against the Communal Award's grant of separate electorates for the Depressed Classes — the provision Ambedkar had fought for. Ambedkar capitulated under the threat of Gandhi's death, signed the Poona Pact, and exchanged separate electorates for reserved seats in a joint electorate. He described it as one of the worst defeats of his political life and spent the rest of his career arguing that Congress treated Dalit welfare as expendable when it conflicted with broader Hindu political interests.

The Grammar of Anarchy passage is addressed, at least in part, to the party that had built its legitimacy through mass civil disobedience and was about to govern the country. You used the street to get here. You cannot use it to stay.

The premise that doesn't hold

The whole argument depends on constitutional methods being open. Ambedkar states this as a fact. It was not straightforwardly one. Consider, as an illustrative case, a Dalit agricultural laborer in Bihar: he had the vote, and he had a landlord who controlled his tenancy, his employment, and his access to credit, and who could stand near the polling station watching who entered. The formal availability of constitutional channels and their practical accessibility were different things. Ambedkar had spent three decades trying to use those channels for Untouchable communities, and had seen the limits of what they could deliver against entrenched social and economic power.

He does not address it. A real tension remains in the argument: Ambedkar insists constitutional methods are now open, yet the social realities of caste and economic dependence severely limited what those methods could deliver for Dalits and the rural poor. The speech does not resolve this — it states the premise and moves on. Whether that reflects a gap in the argument or a decision to hold the contradiction in suspension is something the speech itself cannot settle.

WARNING FOUR
Bhakti in politics — hero worship
The warning
"In India, Bhakti or what may be called the path of devotion or hero-worship, plays a part in its politics unequalled in magnitude by the part it plays in the politics of any other country in the world. Bhakti in religion may be a road to the salvation of the soul. But in politics, Bhakti or hero-worship is a sure road to degradation and to eventual dictatorship."
Constituent Assembly Debates, Vol. XI, 25 November 1949
What Bhakti means

Bhakti is not admiration or respect. It is surrender — the dissolution of the evaluating self into the object of devotion. In the traditions Ambedkar is invoking, the devotee does not assess whether the deity's actions are wise. That distance is what Bhakti closes. You do not measure. You follow. The capacity for critical judgment is not suspended — it is replaced by the certainty of love, which feels like a higher form of knowing.

Applied to political leaders, this destroys accountability from the inside. The great man is not held to the Constitution. He is revered. His decisions are accepted because he made them. The machinery of democratic constraint — courts, opposition, elections — is replaced by a single mechanism: personal devotion. And devotion cannot hold power to account, because doing so requires the ability to say the person you are devoted to is wrong. Bhakti makes that impossible by design.

Ambedkar quotes Mill: do not lay your liberties at the feet of even a great man. The "even" is the point. He is not warning about bad leaders — those are easy. He is warning about genuinely great ones, whose qualities are real, whose service is real, whose greatness is not in question. The danger is not misplaced devotion. It is devotion itself, which does not distinguish between leaders who deserve it and leaders who merely arrive after them.

The problem Ambedkar cannot escape

At the moment he delivered this speech, Ambedkar was already the object of what he was warning against. He still is. Statues, portraits, constitutions carried in protests with his face on the cover — the visual culture of Ambedkarite politics has the character of Bhakti. He knew this. The warning is therefore also self-directed: do not treat me this way. Do not let love of the author become a reason to stop reading the text critically.

But Ambedkar had no control over what happened after his death. And the most devoted Ambedkarites are sometimes the least willing to acknowledge the contradictions in his own positions — which sits in direct tension with the warning he made in this speech.

WARNING FIVE
A life of contradictions
The warning
"On the 26th of January 1950, we are going to enter into a life of contradictions. In politics we will have equality and in social and economic life we will have inequality... If we continue to deny it for long, we will do so only by putting our political democracy in peril. We must remove this contradiction at the earliest possible moment or else those who suffer from inequality will blow up the structure of political democracy which this Assembly has so laboriously built up."
Constituent Assembly Debates, Vol. XI, 25 November 1949
The claim

Political democracy and deep social inequality are, Ambedkar implies, in a relationship of fundamental tension. This is not only a warning about what might go wrong under adverse conditions. It is a structural claim: the logic of political democracy — that every person's interests count equally — and the logic of graded social hierarchy contradict each other, and that contradiction cannot be indefinitely managed.

The distinction Ambedkar draws is exact. "One man one vote and one vote one value" is not the same as "one man one value." Electoral equality — every adult gets a vote — does not produce political equality. In a society built on graded caste hierarchy and extreme poverty, formal voting rights do not translate into equal political influence. The landlord and the landless laborer both hold one vote. They do not hold equivalent power, because political power is not only the ballot — it is everything surrounding the ballot: freedom from economic coercion when casting it, access to candidates who represent your interests, confidence that your constitutional rights will actually be enforced. The Constitution gave everyone the first kind of equality. The second kind was a condition of society that no document could simply declare into existence.

What "blow up" means

Ambedkar says those who suffer from inequality will blow up the structure of political democracy. He does not say how, and that vagueness is deliberate. Violent revolution is one possibility. Electing leaders who promise social equality by dismantling democratic constraints is another — the authoritarian bargain, taken voluntarily by people who have concluded that democratic friction is what stands between them and actual change. Quiet withdrawal — simply stopping to participate or to defend a system that has never worked for you — is a third. All three are the same thing: people deciding the structure is not worth protecting because it has not protected them.

The warning is not falsified by the absence of revolution. The pressure can release through the ballot itself, through elections that produce governments committed to dismantling the institutions that have served only the privileged. Ambedkar knew this. He left "blow up" vague because the form the release takes is genuinely unknowable in advance.

Coats of paint

After the "life of contradictions" passage, Ambedkar turns to fraternity. The Bryce story — Americans at a church convention who could not agree to pray for "our nation" because they did not yet feel themselves to be one — is his illustration of what constitutional rights look like without social solidarity underneath them. You can write equality into a constitution and enforce it through courts. But if people do not actually experience each other as fellow citizens with equivalent standing — if the social reality of village, workplace, and marriage market continues to run on caste — then constitutional equality sits on a surface that is not prepared to hold it. It will not penetrate. It peels.

India's problem is harder than America's — this is Ambedkar's own framing, not an endorsement of the comparison at face value. In his telling, America's difficulty was national and regional solidarity; India's is caste, which generates not merely hierarchy but "jealousy and antipathy between caste and caste" at every level of the structure. The web of suspicion runs throughout, not only from top to bottom. Building fraternity in that context is not a matter of passing the right laws. Ambedkar does not pretend otherwise.

Why this warning is the root

Every other warning in this speech is a consequence of this one. Communities place creed above country partly because the constitutional order has not delivered what it promised — the community is the only loyalty that has actually produced anything concrete. Democracy gives place to dictatorship because a dominant party can offer social delivery that the friction of competitive democracy cannot seem to match. Constitutional methods feel closed to communities for whom they have never worked. The leader who promises to cut through hierarchy accumulates devotion because institutions have not cut through it. And people grow indifferent to government by the people because government by the people has not been government for them.

The six warnings are not a list. They are a single argument about what happens when you build political democracy on a social foundation that contradicts it. This warning names the foundation. The others describe what it produces.

WARNING SIX
Tired of government by the people
The warning
"They are getting tired of Government by the people. They are prepared to have Government for the people and are indifferent whether it is Government of the people and by the people. If we wish to preserve the Constitution in which we have sought to enshrine the principle of Government of the people, for the people and by the people, let us resolve not to be tardy in the recognition of the evils that lie across our path and which induce people to prefer Government for the people to Government by the people."
Constituent Assembly Debates, Vol. XI, 25 November 1949
What this describes

Not rejection. Indifference. People are not turning against democracy — they are ceasing to care about its procedural conditions. They still want government that delivers. But they are becoming indifferent to whether that government is also accountable, whether the opposition is genuinely protected, whether the courts are truly independent. The demand for participation — that government derive its legitimacy from the governed — gradually relaxes into a preference for results. The elections can continue. Just do not ask us to care deeply about whether they are clean.

The authoritarian bargain at the end of this road is not imposed by force. It is accepted voluntarily by people who have decided that democratic friction — the slowness, the accountability mechanisms, the protected opposition — is a cost they are no longer willing to pay. This calculus changes when democracy has not delivered. People who have waited a long time for a process to work become willing to trade the process for the outcome.

What produces it

Ambedkar names the cause directly: "the evils that lie across our path which induce people to prefer Government for the people to Government by the people." He does not list them here because the previous three pages have listed them — caste hierarchy, poverty, the gap between what the Constitution promises and what Indian society delivers. When people's experience of democracy is that it has maintained the conditions of their subjugation, they stop treating democratic process as something worth defending. They ask what it has ever done for them. If the answer is not much, a leader who offers to simply cut through it becomes appealing. That is not irrationality. It is a reasonable response to a long history of disappointment.

The speech implies you cannot ask people to defend a process that has consistently failed them. The warning is not a criticism of those people. It is directed at the political class: remove the conditions that make indifference rational, or — the implication is clear — you will have no standing to be surprised when it arrives.

How the speech ends

The last line: "That is the only way to serve the country. I know of no better." No program. No confidence. No prediction that India will succeed. Ambedkar has written the Constitution and named six ways it can be destroyed. His prescription is one sentence: recognize the conditions that produce democratic failure and remove them.

He was a lawyer and an economist who spent his career producing detailed proposals. The speech's genre here is valedictory, not programmatic — he is not drafting a scheme but handing over a responsibility. What the speech implies is that the problem he has diagnosed is not solvable by constitutional design alone. He has done what a constitution can do. Whether Indians will place country above creed, resist hero worship, close the gap between the vote and the life — none of that can be written into a document.

"I know of no better." Not: I am confident. Not: follow these principles and the democracy will survive. Just: this is the only path I can see. He hands the Constitution over, says what it requires, and leaves the question of whether it will be honored or betrayed to everyone who comes after him.
